About Beyond Diagnosis

What this blog is, how we work, and how to pitch us.

What This Blog Is

Beyond Diagnosis is the blog of the NYC-DSA Healthcare Working Group. We are a vehicle for the working group to communicate with the public about the DSA's positions on healthcare policy and our reaction to healthcare news and events. We also aim to be a starting point for the non-DSA layperson to understand the healthcare landscape — and what socialist policies could offer for their daily lives.

The name reflects our conviction that diagnosing the failures of the for-profit healthcare system, however necessary, is not enough. Every piece we publish is oriented toward solutions: what a better system looks like, how we get there, and what working people can do right now to build the power to win it.

We are not a neutral publication. We are socialists who believe healthcare is a human right, that the current system exists to extract profit from sick people, and that the only path to transformation runs through organized working-class power. We are upfront about that. We also hold ourselves to high standards of accuracy, evidence, and intellectual honesty — because our arguments are stronger when they are true.

Types of Articles

There are a few general categories of articles that are a good fit for the blog. Those include, but are not limited to:

  • Explanatory articles about a facet of our healthcare system, a healthcare policy, or a DSA action. These are "evergreen" and can be published at any time.
  • Reactions to healthcare news or new policy. These should be submitted within 2–3 days of the news occurring.
  • Novel research or analysis done by the healthcare working group or a working group member, including methodology, graphics, and data.
  • Commentary on an article or piece of research published at another outlet or journal.
  • Personal patient and/or healthcare worker stories about a failure of the healthcare system.

Every article, regardless of type, should leave readers with a clear sense of why the subject matters and what they can do to take action in response.

Writing Guidelines

Articles should be written for the average layperson. Assume your reader is smart, but not an expert on healthcare or healthcare policy. Language should be clear and jargon-free. Articles should be 600–800 words.

Articles should be written by humans, not by generative AI. All sources must be clearly cited. Plagiarism will not be tolerated.

All articles should address:

  • What happened or what the topic is
  • Why it matters for the average person's health and daily life
  • How to understand it through a socialist lens
  • What the reader can do in response
  • Links to additional reading for those who want to go deeper

Editorial Standards

All posts are reviewed by blog leadership before publication. After a pitch is accepted, you'll be paired with another working group member to discuss and refine your idea. After writing, articles go through peer review by another member and final editing by blog leadership. Contributors retain ownership of their work. We may edit for length, clarity, and style, and will communicate any substantive changes before publishing.

Pitch Us

Please reach out to Joe Patrick and Tim Frasca if you have an idea for an article or blog post. Your pitch should include:

  • What you want to write about and a suggested headline
  • Why you want to write about this topic now
  • Why you are the best person to write about this specific topic

Pitches will be evaluated based on relevancy, fit with other ongoing articles, and consistency with the stated positions of the working group. We aim to respond within two weeks.
